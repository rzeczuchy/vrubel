The art of Mikhail Vrubel

Mikail Vrubel [Михаил Александрович Врубель] - the 19th-20th century Russian painter

Mikhail Vrubel

Born a fragile and unhealthy child, Mikhail Vrubel displayed artistic inclinations early, but only decided to pursue a career as an artist in his mid-twenties, when he started attending the Imperial Academy of Arts.

In his work he took inspiration from religious art, Russian romantic painters (such as Ivan Aivazovsky), as well as theatre and literature. Despite obvious talent and some acclaim among his peers, Vrubel never graduated from the Academy. Instead he went on to pursue a commercial career as both a religious painter and portretist. His most productive years followed, during which the artists painted some of his most stunning works, including the "Demon" series.

His late years were marred by mental illness and depression, and Vrubel was then hardly able to paint. Even so, a couple of his better-known works (like the Six-winged Seraph, 1904) were produced during this period, indicating his artistic senses were still sharp at times. He left behind a legacy that was rare and defiant.

Paintings

The Demon Seated, 1890

The Demon Seated

Inspired by a 1839 romantic poem by Mikhail Lermontov, it depicts the brooding Demon sitting alone amongst the mountains of the Caucassus. This was one of a series of paintings depicting Lermontov's Demon that Vrubel produced between year 1890 and his death in 1910.

The Swan Princess, 1900

The Swan Princess

Based on The Tale of Tsar Sultan opera by Rimsky-Korsakov, for which Vrubel himself designed the costumes. The part of the Swan Princess was played by Nadezhda Zabela-Vrubel, Mikhail's wife. Although some point out that the face in the painting does not resemble that of his spouse closely, it is similar to the artist's other idealised depictions of his wife.

Vrubel met Nadezhda in 1896, during her rehearsal in the opera, and apparently was instantly enamoured, proposing shortly after to the much younger singer. Their relationship was short-lived, as the artist was kept from seeing his wife in the latter part of his life, due to his deteriorating mental condition.

The Demon Downcast, ca. 1901-1902

The Demon Downcast

Although based around the same topic as the 1890's The Demon Seated, this entry in the "Demon" series seems to constitute a departure from the literary theme of Lermontov's poem. It depicts the Demon lying on the ground in a contorted position, his expression terrifying and full of anguish. By the time the painting was finished, Vrubel was already suffering heavily from the symptoms of his mental illness.

Six-winged Seraph (Azrael)

Alternatively titled Azrael after the Angel of Death from Jewish/ Islamic tradition, the fierce Seraph holds up a dagger in one hand and a half-empty hourglass in another.

Inspired in turn by Alexander Pushkin's The Prophet, this 131x155cm painting was one of the last large-scale works Vrubel produced. At the time, the previously negative public reception of his style was starting to reverse. For the two years of his career as an artist, Vrubel's work finally enjoyed some much deserved recognition.
